Applying Elbers: Local segregation

Alan Engel
Last update: 18 December 2021

Elbers (2021) details how an increase in the share of women working in production and crafts (U.S.) leads to an expected increase in segregation and explains this increase using the example of carpenters. Here, I apply this approach to specialist and technical workers in Japan using the example of designers.

Between 1985 and 2005 the share of women designers rose from 40.3% to 42.5%. But, as shown in the figure, local segregation of designers also increased. (This figure was computed using the function mutual_local with bootstrap error estimation.) In 1985, the female share of this occupation was 40.3% while the female share of the major group was 40.1%. This leads to a local segregation score for designers (with specialist and technical workers) of \(0.403\cdot log(\frac{0.0403}{0.401}})\)
